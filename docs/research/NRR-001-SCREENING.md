# Quantitative Stock Screening for Borsa İstanbul: A Source-Grounded Methodology

## TL;DR
- **Use sequential filtering with rank-composite tie-breaking, not a weight-optimized composite score.** Weight optimization is the single largest avoidable source of overfitting; the academic and practitioner record (DeMiguel-Garlappi-Uppal 2009; the mean-variance optimization literature) shows equal-weighting and simple gating beat optimized weights out-of-sample, especially in noisy emerging-market data. Your decision to abandon the weighted-sum architecture was correct — but sequential filtering has real pitfalls (order dependence, empty/thin survivor sets) you must engineer around.
- **For BIST specifically: measure momentum/trend as relative strength vs. XU100 (not absolute nominal returns), gate the whole screen on a market-regime filter (XU100 vs. 200-day MA + breadth), and treat foreign-custody (yabancı takas) flow as a corroborating tilt, not a primary alpha signal** — the best academic evidence (Ülkü & İkizlerli, 2012) is that foreign flows forecast the aggregate market, not individual stock returns.
- **The two things most likely to invalidate your backtest are look-ahead bias on takas/financial data (T+2 settlement lag + post-close publication) and survivorship bias from halted/transferred names (KOZAA/KOZAL/IPEKE).** Build a point-in-time universe, lag every fundamental and takas input, and include delisted/halted stocks.

## Key Findings

### 1. Which factors actually work in emerging markets
- **Momentum/relative strength: the most durable cross-sectional factor in EM, and the standout of 2024–2025.** Per State Street Global Advisors, "In 2024, Momentum ranked as the best-performing factor for the US, developed ex-US, and emerging market regions for the fourth time in the past 20 years," with US momentum's rolling 12-month excess return reaching the 96th percentile over 50 years — a signal that the factor was also running hot/crowded, which argues for regime-gating rather than naive chasing.
- **Low-volatility: strong and arguably the most robust EM factor, with the bonus of low turnover.** Blitz, Pang & van Vliet, "The Volatility Effect in Emerging Markets" (*Emerging Markets Review* 16, 2013, pp. 31–45), found across 30 emerging-market countries (1988–2010) that "the relation between risk and return is flat, or even negative," with an annual low-minus-high-volatility spread of 2.1%; the effect is investable in liquid large-caps and has low turnover — ideal for a cost-sensitive BIST screen.
- **Value: more resilient in EM than in developed markets**, where it suffered the "quant winter."
- **Quality: positive but the weakest-documented in EM**; evidence is thinner and more sensitive to definition.
- **Decayed/fragile: size, and momentum/quality in some single-country samples (e.g., China A-shares, where size and value survive but momentum and quality do not).** Momentum specifically has historically failed on BIST in several samples (see §3).

### 2. Factor combination — the critical question
**Verdict: sequential filtering (a funnel) is more robust to overfitting than a weighted composite score for your use case, but it is not free of problems. Use a hybrid: hard sequential gates for non-negotiable conditions (liquidity, regime, price-limit tradeability), then an equal-weighted rank composite among survivors for ranking — never an optimized weight vector.**

Why weighted-composite-score + weight optimization overfits:
- **Optimization amplifies estimation error.** Best & Grauer (1991) and the broader mean-variance literature show optimization "amplifies estimation errors"; out-of-sample, equal weights routinely beat "optimized" weights.
- **Equal-weighting beats optimized weights out-of-sample in noisy data.** A large body of evidence since the 1970s (Dawes & Corrigan 1974) shows regression-derived weights forecast worse than equal weights when data is noisy and predictors are many. The canonical finance result is DeMiguel, Garlappi & Uppal (2009, *Review of Financial Studies* 22(5):1915–1953): across seven datasets, none of 14 optimized mean-variance models consistently beat a naive 1/N portfolio out-of-sample, because "estimation error in the inputs was large enough to wipe out their theoretical advantage." This is exactly the EM factor setting.
- **In EM specifically, optimization almost never wins.** In one survey of 96 implementations, mean-variance optimization beat equal weighting in 45/48 European tests but only 4/48 emerging-market tests (2002–2010, net of trading costs).
- **Compositing washes out signal by averaging.** Summing z-scores lets a strong signal on one factor be diluted by middling scores on others; the composite regresses toward the cross-sectional mean.
- **Z-score weighting creates instability.** Z-scores are sensitive to outliers and to the estimation window for mean/standard deviation; small changes in the normalization window can reorder the top-N — a hidden free parameter that invites overfitting.

Sequential filtering's own pitfalls (stated honestly):
- **Order dependence / path dependence** — different filter orderings yield different survivor sets when filters are not pure AND-conditions.
- **Risk of too few (or zero) survivors** — a tight funnel can return an empty set in a bad regime (this is actually informative, see §5).
- The screening literature confirms sequential ("conjunctive") screens maximize specificity at the cost of sensitivity, and that ordering/thresholds matter — so keep the gates few, economically motivated, and fixed in advance.

### 3. Relative strength / momentum on BIST + the inflation question
- **Measure RS relative to XU100, not as absolute nominal return. This is the single most important BIST design choice and it directly solves the inflation problem.** With Turkish inflation having run very high (policy rate held in the mid-30s% range through 2026, with disinflation only gradually underway), absolute nominal returns are dominated by a common inflationary drift that is roughly identical across all stocks — it adds no cross-sectional information and inflates apparent "momentum." Measuring each stock's return *relative to the index* differences out the common nominal/inflation component, leaving the genuine cross-sectional signal. Relative strength is therefore inherently inflation-robust in a way absolute nominal-return ranking is not. (You do not need to deflate by CPI if you rank relative to the index, because the index carries the same nominal drift.)
- **Practitioner convention:** RS vs. XU100 (and sector-relative), with 3/6/12-month lookbacks and a skip-month (skip the most recent ~21 trading days to avoid short-term reversal). Turkish technical practice also uses Wilder's RSI (14-day) as a separate momentum oscillator, but that is a timing overlay, not the cross-sectional RS factor.
- **BIST caution on momentum:** academic evidence is mixed-to-negative. A Fama–MacBeth cross-sectional study of BIST (Jan 2009–Dec 2015) found momentum and size effects *absent* across all size groups, while book-to-market and dividend yield persisted; a separate 2010s BIST study found that winner portfolios generated *negative* abnormal returns and loser portfolios positive ones (i.e., contrarian/reversal behavior) in most holding periods. Treat momentum as a real but regime-dependent factor on BIST — another reason to gate it with a regime filter and lean on low-vol/value as co-anchors.

### 4. Foreign flow (yabancı takas) as a factor
- **Academic verdict (decisive): net foreign flows forecast the aggregate market, NOT individual stock returns.** Ülkü & İkizlerli (2012), "The interaction between foreigners' trading and emerging stock returns: Evidence from Turkey," *Emerging Markets Review* 13(3), pp. 381–409, document a "positive and lasting relationship" between foreign net buying and aggregate local returns, with permanent (information-based) price impact — but the predictability does not extend to single stocks. Implication: use foreign-custody change as a market-level regime input and a stock-level *corroborating tilt*, not as a primary stock-selection alpha.
- **Data & timing (critical for look-ahead):** custody/takas distribution is published **after the session close**, and because settlement is **T+2**, the published ratio reflects trades from **two business days earlier** (Fintables, Matriks). MKK's free public feed carries an *additional* **10-business-day delay**; only licensed vendors get the post-close (T+2-lagged) feed. The T+1 settlement transition must be complete by **31 December 2026** (test environments from Jan 2026), which will compress the lag to ~1 day.
- **New 2025 granularity:** from **1 January 2025**, BIST's takas data became a licensed product adding genuinely new stock-level detail — **individual-vs-institutional custody split per stock**, number of investors per stock, free-float reports, and custody balances by broker/member.
- **Normalization in practice:** the ratio is foreign-held lots / total circulating lots ×100. Practitioners (Fintables, Osmanlı Menkul) track the **percentage-point change** over daily/weekly/monthly and **1-3-6 month** windows; the watched signal is sustained 2–3 month foreign buying, ideally with rising volume and index membership (BIST 30/50 most reliable). You must control for float changes (bedelli/bedelsiz capital increases mechanically distort the ratio). There is **no formal z-score convention among Turkish brokers** — a z-score of the percentage-point change is your own (reasonable) framing, valid only if computed on point-in-time, lag-respecting data.

### 5. Output: fixed top-N vs threshold
- **Use threshold-gated, capped top-N.** Apply absolute hurdles (regime ON, RS percentile, liquidity, takas not collapsing) first; then rank survivors and take up to N. If fewer than N clear the hurdles, output fewer — an empty or thin set in a bad regime is honest information, not a failure. Pure fixed-N always fills the book even in a bear market (false comfort); pure threshold can be empty. The cap+floor hybrid captures both and is the more intellectually honest design given your human+LLM review layer.

### 6. Validation — concrete implementation
**(a) Information Coefficient (IC) and rank-IC.** For each rebalance date *t*, compute the cross-sectional correlation between each stock's signal and its *forward* return over the holding horizon *h*:
- Pearson IC_t = corr(signal_i,t, fwdret_i,t+h); **rank-IC_t = Spearman** = corr(rank(signal_i,t), rank(fwdret_i,t+h)). **Prefer rank-IC** — it is robust to outliers and price-limit spikes (Spearman on ranks).
- Average across dates: mean IC. **ICIR = mean(IC) / std(IC)** across time. Benchmarks: single-name equity ICs typically fall in ~0.02–0.10; **ICIR > 0.5 = good consistency, > 1.0 = strong**. A daily rank-IC of ~0.03 is genuinely meaningful in a noisy market, not trivial.
- Check **IC decay**: compute IC at h = 1, 5, 10, 21, 63 days to find where predictive content dies — this sets your rebalance frequency and the skip-month convention. Winsorize (e.g., 3σ or MAD) and neutralize for sector/size before computing IC to avoid a single sector driving the result.

**(b) Quantile spread / monotonicity.** Sort the universe into quantiles; given ~100 names, use **quintiles, not deciles**, for statistical stability (~20 names per bucket). Form equal-weighted quantile portfolios, hold *h*, rebalance, and test that mean forward return is **monotonic** across Q1→Q5. The economically meaningful test is the **top-minus-bottom (Q1−Q5) spread**, but require *monotonicity*, not just a positive spread: a positive but non-monotonic pattern signals an unstable signal. Use a formal monotonicity test (Patton–Timmermann style) rather than eyeballing.

**(c) Look-ahead pitfalls — BIST-specific.**
- **Takas:** today's published ratio reflects trades 2 business days ago and is released post-close — never act on takas at a price from before its real availability. Lag it ≥1 trading day in backtests (≥2 to be safe pre-T+1); if you only have the free MKK feed, lag ~10 business days to match what you can actually trade on.
- **Financials (KAP):** quarterly statements have legally defined maximum filing dates well after period-end. For FY2025: non-consolidated by **2 March 2026**, consolidated by **11 March 2026**. Q1-2026: non-consolidated by **30 April 2026**, consolidated by **11 May 2026** (banks slightly later). Disclosures must be made outside session hours. Use the actual KAP disclosure date (point-in-time stamp), not period-end.
- Build a **point-in-time** universe and as-reported fundamentals; never use restated data.

**(d) Survivorship bias — the KOZAA/KOZAL example.** The Koza–İpek group (KOZAL, KOZAA, IPEKE) was placed under TMSF trusteeship (2016), later transferred via the Treasury to the Türkiye Wealth Fund — and crucially **kept trading on BIST** while posting extreme, governance-driven returns that far outran the index (KOZAL was renamed Türk Altın İşletmeleri A.Ş., ticker TRALT, in November 2025). This is a double trap: (1) names that halt/delist entirely vanish from a naive current-constituent universe (upward bias); (2) names with idiosyncratic special-situation moves wildly distort momentum/RS and will be repeatedly "selected" by a backtest that includes them with hindsight. Build the backtest universe from the **historical constituent list at each date**, include halted/delisted names with their final (or delisting) returns, and apply the tradeability filter below.

**(e) Transaction costs.** BIST retail commissions run roughly 0.1–0.3% per side (e.g., Garanti BBVA default 0.195% / max 0.32%; Halk Yatırım standard 0.3%; deep-discount online tiers lower) **plus 5% BSMV levied on the commission** (not on principal), plus the bid-ask spread (wide in small caps). A round-trip on the order of ~1.5% materially degrades high-turnover factor strategies; the China A-share analogy (~1.5% round-trip "destroys high-turnover strategies") applies directly. Favor lower-turnover signals (low-vol, 6–12m RS), lengthen holding/rebalance periods, and **model costs explicitly in the quintile backtest** — report the cost-adjusted Q1−Q5 spread, not the gross one.

## Details

### BIST microstructure essentials
- **Price limits / circuit breakers:** ±10% daily price limit on equities in BIST Stars/Main (relative to a reference price, typically prior-session VWAP / last call-auction price); a stock-specific circuit breaker halts continuous trading and moves to a single-price call auction when intraday moves hit thresholds. The **index** circuit breaker moved from a two-stage (5%/7%) system to a **single-stage 6% daily-decline halt effective 1 September 2025**, with the cooling-off period cut by 10 minutes and the short-selling uptick trigger lowered from 3% to 2% on BIST 100 (Türkiye Today, Aug 2025).
  - **Screening implications:** (1) a stock that closed limit-up cannot be entered at the screen price next morning — exclude or flag names that closed at/near the limit; (2) limit moves and halts create gappy, non-continuous prices that bias momentum/RS and volatility estimates; (3) illiquid small caps that ratchet through consecutive limit days produce *fake* momentum you can't actually trade. Add a "tradeable next-open" filter.
- **Liquidity filter (ADV):** the BIST universe spans from highly liquid BIST 30 names to near-untradeable small caps; Borsa İstanbul has ranked among the highest share-turnover-velocity markets globally. Impose a hard **TL average-daily-value floor** over the trailing 20–60 days so the screen only surfaces names you can enter/exit at your size without moving price. Apply this as the first sequential gate.
- **Settlement:** currently T+2; moving to T+1 by end-2026 — re-baseline takas-lag assumptions when it goes live.

### Market-regime gating for BIST
- Use **XU100 vs. its 200-day moving average** as the primary regime switch; trend/moving-average regime filters are well-documented to cut drawdowns in momentum systems. Corroborate with **breadth** (% of constituents above their 50-day MA) and trend-strength (50- vs 200-day slope, ADX). When regime is OFF (XU100 below 200-day MA and/or deteriorating breadth), tighten the funnel or move to cash — momentum/RS strategies suffer their worst crashes in down regimes, and BIST momentum is specifically regime-dependent (§3).

### Recommended architecture (deterministic)
1. **Hard gates (sequential AND-conditions, order-insensitive):** ADV floor; tradeable-next-open (not limit-locked); minimum price/float; data freshness (point-in-time, lagged takas & KAP dates).
2. **Regime gate:** XU100 > 200-day MA AND healthy breadth → full screen; else reduce N / raise hurdles / cash.
3. **Equal-weighted rank composite among survivors:** RS vs. XU100 (6- & 12-month, skip 1-month), low-volatility rank, plus a value or quality rank *only if* it validates on point-in-time data. Average the **ranks** (not z-scores), equal weights.
4. **Foreign-takas tilt (corroborating only):** require foreign-custody % not collapsing over trailing weeks; use as a tie-breaker, never a primary score.
5. **Output:** threshold-gated, capped top-5 to top-10; allow fewer in weak regimes.
6. **Human + LLM commentary** layered on the deterministic shortlist.

## Recommendations
1. **Immediately:** lock in RS-vs-XU100 (relative, not absolute nominal) as the trend core — this resolves the inflation distortion at the design level. Add the regime gate and ADV floor as hard sequential filters.
2. **Replace any remaining z-score weighting with equal-weighted rank averaging among survivors.** Do not optimize weights. If you must tilt, do it via hard gates, not continuous weights.
3. **Build a point-in-time dataset:** lagged takas (≥2 trading days pre-T+1, or ~10 business days if on the free MKK feed), KAP disclosure-date-stamped fundamentals, and a historical-constituent universe that includes halted/transferred names (the KOZAA/KOZAL/IPEKE/TRALT class).
4. **Validate in this order:** rank-IC + ICIR per factor → IC decay curve (sets rebalance frequency and skip-month) → quintile monotonicity + Q1−Q5 spread **net of costs** (commission + 5% BSMV + spread).
5. **Thresholds that change the plan:** if a factor's rank-IC is not consistently positive (ICIR < ~0.3) on point-in-time data, drop it. If the cost-adjusted quintile spread is non-monotonic or negative, treat the signal as noise. If momentum's rank-IC flips negative in OFF regimes, gate it harder or disable it there.
6. **Re-test after the T+1 transition (end-2026):** the takas lag compresses to ~1 day, changing the usable freshness of foreign-flow signals.

## Caveats
- **Every approach here is imperfect, including the recommended one.** The hybrid funnel still embeds order/threshold choices that are themselves tunable — keep them few, economically motivated, and fixed in advance; do not grid-search them, or you reintroduce the overfitting you eliminated by dropping weight optimization.
- **Foreign-flow stock-level predictability is weak by the best academic evidence** (Ülkü & İkizlerli 2012); do not overweight takas as alpha.
- **BIST momentum is fragile and regime-dependent**; the durable-factor case rests more on low-volatility and value than on momentum in this specific market — multiple BIST studies find momentum absent or reversed.
- **Free MKK takas data is delayed ~10 business days**; without a licensed feed your live signal is materially staler than a naive backtest assumes — match backtest lag to the feed you will actually trade on.
- **Factor crowding/decay is real** — momentum's premium has compressed over decades and ran historically hot/crowded in 2024; persistence is best-supported for low-vol and value, and for *relative* (not absolute) construction.
- The Koza "+533% vs ~150% index" outperformance figure circulating in Turkish financial press could not be verified against an authoritative source and is excluded; the qualitative point (extreme, special-situation-driven outperformance that distorts a backtest) stands regardless.
- Several practitioner sources cited are broker/vendor marketing or retail-education pages; their normalization conventions describe practice, not validated alpha claims — treat them as "how it's measured," with the academic layer carrying "whether it works."
